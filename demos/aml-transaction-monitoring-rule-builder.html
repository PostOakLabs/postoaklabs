BSA · FinCEN · FATF — why this matters

U.S. BSA 31 CFR §1020.210 requires every covered institution to have a risk-based AML programme that includes a transaction-monitoring function. FinCEN's 2026 AML/CFT Priorities name corruption, cyber-enabled crime, fraud, transnational crime, drug trafficking, human trafficking and proliferation finance — TM rules are how each one becomes detectable. The EU AML Single Rulebook (Regulation 2024/1624) harmonises the equivalent obligation across member states from 2027.

Sources: 31 CFR §1020.210 · FinCEN AML/CFT National Priorities · EU 2024/1624 (AMLR) · FATF R.10–R.21

Value at risk · TM programme inadequacy

Recent FinCEN / DOJ consent orders for TM failures range $300K → $140M+ per institution

FinCEN consent orders citing weak transaction-monitoring (TD Bank 2024, Bittrex 2022, Capital One 2021, USAA 2022) imposed penalties from low-eight to high-nine figures. The recurring theme: under-tuned thresholds let typologies go undetected; over-tuned thresholds flooded analysts with alerts and led to missed SARs. Calibrating the trade-off explicitly — and binding the chosen thresholds into a machine-readable mandate the runtime cannot silently relax — is the audit-grade response.
